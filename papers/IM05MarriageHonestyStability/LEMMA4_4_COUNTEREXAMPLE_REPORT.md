# Counterexample to Lemma 4.4 in Immorlica--Mahdian

This note records a finite counterexample to Lemma 4.4 as stated in
Immorlica and Mahdian, "Marriage, Honesty, and Stability."  It is written so
that the issue can be checked from the paper PDF and the arithmetic below.

## Where to Check the Paper

The relevant source assumptions are in the setup of Theorem 3.1.  The paper
allows an arbitrary fixed distribution `D` over women, with every woman having
positive probability.  A man's length-`k` preference list is formed by drawing
from `D` repeatedly and ignoring repetitions until `k` distinct women have
been obtained.  Equivalently, this is the Plackett--Luce top-`k` sampler with
weights proportional to the probabilities in `D`.

In Section 4.3, just before Lemma 4.2, the paper defines `E_w` as the event
that woman `w` does not appear on any man's preference list, and defines
`Y_g` as the number of women `w <= g` for which `E_w` happens.

Lemma 4.4 states that

```text
Var(Y_g) <= E[Y_g].
```

The proof tries to establish this by showing the events `E_i` are pairwise
negatively correlated.  It reduces the claim to the one-man events `F_i`,
where `F_i` is the event that a given man does not include woman `i`, and then
claims it is enough to show

```text
Pr[F_i | F_j] <= Pr[F_i]
```

for every pair `i, j`.

The problematic step is the multiset/permutation argument in the proof of
Lemma 4.4.  The paper conditions on `F_j` and then treats this as equivalent
to deleting all copies of woman `j` from the multiset used to simulate the
random list.  That equivalence is false for nonuniform `D`.

## Counterexample Instance

Take five men and five women.  Let `k = 3`.  Let the five women be

```text
a, b, c, d, e
```

and let the distribution `D` be proportional to the weights

```text
a: 50
b: 50
c: 1
d: 1
e: 1
```

Thus the total weight is `103`.  Every woman has positive probability, so this
is within the paper's stated model.  A man's preference list is generated by
drawing three distinct women without replacement, where at each step the next
woman is chosen with probability proportional to her remaining weight.

Let `A_a` be the one-man event that woman `a` is omitted from the man's
length-three list.  Similarly define `A_b`.

## One-Man Omission Probabilities

First compute `p = Pr[A_a]`.  If `a` is omitted, the list consists of three
women drawn from `{b, c, d, e}`.  There are two cases.

The list contains `b` and two of the three low-weight women.  Summing over the
possible positions of `b` gives

```text
6 * 50/(103 * 53 * 52)
+ 6 * 50/(103 * 102 * 52)
+ 6 * 50/(103 * 102 * 101).
```

The list contains only the three low-weight women.  This contributes

```text
6/(103 * 102 * 101).
```

Therefore

```text
p = Pr[A_a] = Pr[A_b] = 461653 / 243700678.
```

Next compute `q = Pr[A_a and A_b]`.  If both popular women are omitted, the
man's list must be exactly `{c, d, e}` in some order.  Hence

```text
q = Pr[A_a and A_b]
  = 6/(103 * 102 * 101)
  = 1/176851.
```

The paper's claimed one-man negative correlation would require `q <= p^2`.
But

```text
q / p^2 = 335819534284 / 213123492409 > 3/2 > 1.
```

Thus

```text
Pr[A_a | A_b] > Pr[A_a].
```

This is the opposite of the conditional comparison used in Lemma 4.4.

## Variance Violation

Now consider all five men.  Let `E_a` be the event that every man omits woman
`a`; define `E_b` similarly.  Since the men's lists are independent,

```text
Pr[E_a] = p^5,
Pr[E_b] = p^5,
Pr[E_a and E_b] = q^5.
```

Order the two weight-50 women first, breaking their tie arbitrarily, and take
`g = 2`.  Then

```text
Y_g = 1_{E_a} + 1_{E_b}.
```

So

```text
E[Y_g] = 2 p^5,
E[Y_g^2] = 2 p^5 + 2 q^5,
Var(Y_g) = E[Y_g^2] - E[Y_g]^2
         = 2 p^5 + 2 q^5 - 4 p^10.
```

Therefore

```text
Var(Y_g) - E[Y_g] = 2 q^5 - 4 p^10.
```

Because `q / p^2 > 3/2`,

```text
q^5 / p^10 > (3/2)^5 = 243/32 > 2.
```

Hence

```text
2 q^5 - 4 p^10 > 0,
```

and so

```text
Var(Y_g) > E[Y_g].
```

This directly contradicts Lemma 4.4 as stated.

## What Exactly Goes Wrong

The paper's multiset argument approximates the distribution `D^k` by taking a
large multiset with many copies of each woman, drawing a random permutation,
and reading off the first `k` distinct names.  That part is reasonable.

The error is the next step: conditioning on woman `j` being absent from the
first `k` distinct names is not equivalent to deleting all copies of `j` from
the multiset before running the process.  In a nonuniform distribution, a
high-weight woman being absent is informative.  Conditional on one high-weight
woman being absent, it can become more likely that another high-weight woman
is also absent.  The calculation above gives an explicit instance of this
phenomenon.

The issue does not depend on incomplete preferences, stability, or strategic
behavior.  It is already present in the one-man random-list distribution used
as input to the proof.

## Is This Fatal for the Main Result?

This is fatal for Lemma 4.4 as stated, and therefore for the published proof
route of the arbitrary-nonuniform case.  The variance lemma is invoked in the
Chebyshev step used to prove Lemma 4.1, so the proof cannot be repaired merely
by filling in the omitted details of the negative-correlation argument.

This counterexample is not, by itself, a disproof of the paper's main
asymptotic theorem.  It is a small finite instance with `n = 5`, `k = 3`, and
`g = 2`.  The main theorem uses the variance control in a larger-`g`
asymptotic regime, together with Lemma 4.3.  A different argument might still
establish enough concentration for that regime, or the theorem might remain
true under the original assumptions.

One repair direction is to replace `Y_g` by a tail count rather than counting
all women up to rank `g`.  Lemma 4.3's final lower-bound sum only uses women in
a lower-popularity tail, after first deriving bounds for ranks beyond the top
`2k`.  The counterexample above lives entirely in the very top block.  Thus a
corrected proof can try to prove concentration only for the tail random
variable actually needed by that lower-bound sum, then feed that corrected
variance statement into the same Chebyshev step.  This would be a deviation
from the printed Lemma 4.4, not a validation of Lemma 4.4 as stated.

What the counterexample does establish is narrower but important:

1. The conditional comparison `Pr[F_i | F_j] <= Pr[F_i]` is false for the
   paper's stated `D^k` model.
2. The variance bound `Var(Y_g) <= E[Y_g]` is false as a standalone finite
   lemma under the paper's stated assumptions.
3. Any proof of the main result for arbitrary nonuniform `D` needs a different
   variance/concentration argument, a restriction on `D`, or a modification of
   the statement being proved.
